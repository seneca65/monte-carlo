How This App Works

What is Monte Carlo?

A Monte Carlo simulation runs your retirement scenario thousands of times, each time using a different random sequence of market returns and inflation. This gives you a realistic picture of what could happen across a wide range of market conditions — good years, bad years, crashes, and recoveries.

This app runs 10,000 trials. Each trial simulates your full retirement (e.g., 30 years) with different random returns each year. The results show you how often your portfolio survived and how much was left at the end.

Understanding the inputs

  • Initial wealth — Your starting portfolio value on the day you retire.
  • Withdrawal % — The percentage of your starting portfolio you plan to withdraw in year one, then inflation-adjusted each year. For example, 3.2% of $1,250,000 = $40,000 year one.
  • Simulation years — How many years to simulate. 30 years is the standard planning horizon. Use 25 for a shorter test.
  • Market return mean — The average annual return you expect from your fund.
  • Market return std dev — How much the return varies year to year. A higher number means more volatility.
  • Inflation mean — The average annual inflation rate you expect.
  • Inflation std dev — How much inflation varies year to year.

How to choose your inputs

The best inputs come from your own fund's actual historical performance. Here is how to calculate them:

  • Gather your fund's annual returns for as many years as possible
  • Calculate the average (mean) of all those annual returns
  • Calculate the standard deviation — this measures how much the returns varied year to year
  • Do the same for inflation using historical CPI data

The inputs currently loaded are based on S&P 500 and CPI data from 1928 through 2025 — 97 years of actual market history including the Great Depression, World War II, the 1970s stagflation, the 2008 crisis, and COVID.

Using your own fund's historical data is better than generic assumptions — it reflects the actual volatility and returns of the investment you hold.

Understanding the results

  • Median end balance — Half of the 10,000 trials ended above this amount, half below. This is the most realistic middle-ground outcome.
  • Average end balance — The mathematical mean of all 10,000 ending balances. Usually higher than the median because a few very good runs pull it up.
  • Std deviation — How spread out the outcomes are. A large number means a wide range of possible results.
  • Ruin rate — The percentage of trials where the portfolio ran out of money before the end of the simulation. You want this as low as possible — ideally under 5%.
  • Best case / Worst case — The single best and worst outcomes across all 10,000 trials.
  • Distribution chart — Shows the shape of all 10,000 outcomes. A curve shifted to the right means most trials ended well.
  • Percentiles — The 10th percentile means 90% of trials did better than this. The 90th percentile means only 10% did better. Use the 10th percentile as your conservative planning number.
  • Survival rate — The percentage of trials where money remained at the end. The complement of the ruin rate.

Tips for getting the most out of this app

  • Run the simulation multiple times — results vary slightly each run because the returns are random. The overall picture should stay consistent.
  • Test different withdrawal rates (3%, 3.2%, 3.5%, 4%) to see how the ruin rate changes.
  • Test different time horizons (25, 30, 35 years) to understand the effect of longevity on your plan.
  • A ruin rate under 5% with a 30-year horizon at your chosen withdrawal rate is generally considered a safe plan.
  • Use the 10th percentile outcome — not the median — as your conservative planning floor.
  • Once you've chosen a withdrawal rate here, use the Endowment Withdrawal Planner app to execute the strategy year by year.

How this app is built

The simulation runs entirely in your browser using JavaScript — no data is sent anywhere, no server is involved, and nothing is stored or tracked. Your numbers stay completely private.

  • Random number generation — Each trial draws returns and inflation from a normal distribution using the Box-Muller algorithm, the same mathematical approach used in professional Monte Carlo tools. The generator is freshly seeded each run so every simulation is independent.
  • 10,000 trials — Each trial simulates the full retirement horizon year by year. Returns and inflation are drawn independently for every year of every trial.
  • Statistics — Mean, median, percentiles, and standard deviation are calculated directly from all 10,000 raw results with no approximation or rounding during calculation.
  • Histogram display — The chart clips the display at the 1st and 99th percentile for readability. All statistics shown above the chart use the full unclipped dataset.
  • Technology — Built with plain HTML, CSS, and JavaScript. No frameworks, no tracking, no ads.
