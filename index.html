What is a Monte Carlo simulation?

Instead of assuming a fixed return every year, the simulator runs 10,000 different retirement scenarios — each with a different random sequence of market returns and inflation rates. At the end, you see what percentage of those scenarios left the portfolio intact. That is your survival rate.

How to read the survival curve

The curve starts at 100% on the left — all 10,000 trials are alive in year one. Each year the line drops as some trials run out of money. A flat line staying near the top is excellent. A steep early drop means the withdrawal rate is too aggressive. The final point on the right is your overall survival rate.

How each trial works

1
Start with your portfolio value.
2
Withdraw the annual amount at the start of each year, adjusted for cumulative inflation.
3
Apply a random market return for the year.
4
Apply a random inflation rate for the year.
5
Repeat for the number of simulation years. If the portfolio hits zero the trial fails.

What survival rate should I aim for?

Most financial planners suggest targeting 85–95%. A 90% rate means 9 out of 10 simulated retirements lasted the full period. Higher is safer but may mean withdrawing less than you could.
