How this works

What is a Monte Carlo simulation?

Instead of assuming a fixed return every year, the simulator runs 10,000 different retirement scenarios, each with a different random sequence of market returns and inflation rates. Some years the market goes up a lot, some years it drops. The simulation captures that real-world randomness.

At the end, you can see what percentage of those 10,000 scenarios left the portfolio intact — that is your survival rate.

How each trial works

1
Start with your portfolio value.
2
At the beginning of each year, withdraw the annual amount (starting withdrawal adjusted for cumulative inflation).
3
Apply a random market return for the year (drawn from a normal distribution using your mean and std dev).
4
Apply a random inflation rate for the year (drawn from a normal distribution using your mean and std dev).
5
Repeat for the number of simulation years. If the portfolio hits zero, the trial fails.
6
After 10,000 trials, count how many survived. That percentage is your survival rate.

What survival rate should I aim for?

Most financial planners suggest targeting 85–95% survival. A 90% rate means that in 9 out of 10 simulated retirements your portfolio lasted the full period. Higher is safer but may mean withdrawing less than you could.

Suggested starting inputs

Mean return: 7.5% Std dev return: 12.0% Mean inflation: 2.5% Std dev inflation: 1.5% Years: 30
